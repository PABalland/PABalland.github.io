Enlargement & EU competitiveness

How can EU enlargement to the Western Balkans Six (Albania, Bosnia & Herzegovina, Kosovo, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Serbia) and the Eastern Neighbourhood (Ukraine, Moldova, Georgia) contribute to strengthening EU competitiveness — across digital/AI, connectivity, labour, critical raw materials, trade & value chains, and defence?

Headline answer

A conditional, channel-specific “yes.” The clearest, best-documented gains are integration gains — these economies are already being wired into EU value chains, transport corridors and labour markets, and enlargement deepens and de-risks that. A smaller set of countries carry the strategic-asset case: critical raw materials (Serbia, Georgia, Kosovo, Albania, Ukraine) and defence-industrial capacity (Ukraine above all). The corpus is net-positive but heavily caveated — 268 supports / 114 qualifies / 1 contradicts — and the sharpest tension is that the labour mobility that helps the EU is the same brain drain that weakens the candidates.

What the evidence says, by channel

1 · Digital, high-tech & AI

Contribution runs through fast-growing, EU-facing ICT-services and software-outsourcing sectors and a young STEM pool. Serbia is the standout — top-five globally in ICT talent (Startup Genome), ICT exports of €1.3bn up 41% y/y, a software-outsourcing boom, and ICT/FDI spillovers making it among Europe’s fastest-growing economies. Kosovo (ICT exports +⅓ 2008–20, young + broadband, 5G agenda), North Macedonia (ICT its most dynamic sector; new Ministry of Digital Transformation) and Georgia (large ICT spillovers, dynamic digital-jobs market) follow. Caveat: R&D is only ~17% of the EU average, innovation output is weak, and AI access is limited — the strength is ICT delivery, not frontier R&D. Ukraine’s digital signal is thin in these macro reports.

2 · Connectivity & infrastructure

Physical integration into the single market via TEN-T and EU-facing corridors, plus energy-market coupling. The WB6 are being knitted in: a dedicated Western Balkans–Eastern Mediterranean corridor extends TEN-T, backed by a €9bn EU pledge; concrete works include BiH’s Corridor Vc and Croatia gas interconnectors, North Macedonia’s Corridors VIII/X, Serbia’s open rail market and 5G cross-border corridor, Montenegro’s Port of Bar / Corridor XI. In the East, Georgia is the Middle Corridor hub offering a non-Russia route, Moldova builds EU-recognised customs (AEO) and Romania rail links, and Ukraine’s food exports transit EU Solidarity Lanes. Caveat: WB6 rail often fails TEN-T standards and projects slip 2+ years; Ukraine’s routes are war-vulnerable.

3 · Labour markets & demographics

The double-edged channel. Young, EU-integrated diasporas and wide wage gaps (Albanian median wage ~15% of EU; Moldovan ~60%) give the EU a mobile skills supply; raising WB6 participation to EU levels would add 2.8m workers, and Montenegro is already a net labour importer. BiH migrants are well-integrated into EU labour markets; Kosovo is “one of the youngest countries in an aging Europe.” But the same emigration drives declining, ageing workforces and a “labor-market paradox” (shortages alongside >10% unemployment), and EU visa liberalisation accelerates youth outflow. Most rows here are coded qualifies: the EU gain is partly a transfer that weakens the source economies.

4 · Critical raw materials

Concentrated, concrete, strategically central. Serbia: large copper/gold (Cukaru Peki) and lithium/borate (Jadar) deposits, rising mining FDI. Georgia: a consistent copper exporter, EU exports “dominated by raw materials such as copper ores.” Kosovo: rich in lead, zinc, nickel, cobalt, bauxite, manganese; nickel exports resumed 2024. Albania: chrome and copper — with potential copper-wire production for EV motors. BiH: iron ore, bauxite, lead, zinc, copper. Ukraine: a large metals/mining base, but heavily war-damaged. Caveat: much is exported as low-value raw material. Gaps: no CRM evidence for North Macedonia or Moldova.

5 · Trade & value chains

The densest channel. The European automotive/electrical value chain runs through the whole set — Albania’s auto-parts, Moldova’s wire harnesses for European brands, North Macedonia’s exports half-linked to the German automotive market, Serbia/North Macedonia in EU-centric manufacturing GVCs. FDI is large and EU-facing (Serbia takes >½ of WB6 FDI, at 4× the EU’s FDI/GDP ratio; the EU is the top partner for most). Nearshoring to “reduce dependence on single countries” is explicit for Moldova and Kosovo; Ukraine is an EU agri-food and upstream-materials supplier. Caveat: value-added is often low, nearshoring gains may be short-lived, and CBAM exposure in steel/aluminium/fertiliser is largely unassessed.

6 · Defence industrial base

Thin in these sources (as expected) but strategically significant. Ukraine is weightiest — expanding domestic defence production, industry reoriented to defence, and defence-tech innovation via the BRAVE1 platform. Serbia manufactures weapons/military equipment with active arms-export deals; BiH has arms production and dual-use metal-parts capacity; Albania’s MoD trades military goods; Montenegro (NATO) is raising defence spend toward 5% of GDP. Gap: no defence-industrial evidence for Kosovo, North Macedonia, Moldova, Georgia — a limit of the source type, not necessarily reality; a defence-specific review would be needed to close it.

Method & how to verify

A pre-registered, replayable pipeline. Discovery: World Bank WDS API + targeted search of IMF eLibrary and the OECD content-DAM CDN (the HTML sites are bot-blocked; the PDFs are open). A frozen protocol fixed the inclusion rules and per-sector coding definitions before collection. A deterministic scan flagged sector-relevant pages; evidence was coded to verbatim quotes; then every quote was re-opened at its cited PDF page and dropped if not found (383/383 passed).

Audit chain for any claim:  evidence row → doc + PDF page + verbatim quote → pdf_url + sha256 → discovery query.

Coverage & limits

  • Logged absences (verified): Ukraine has no Article IV (wartime EFF reviews only); no IMF Selected Issues for Kosovo, Montenegro, Moldova; Ukraine is the only one of the nine with a standalone OECD Economic Survey — the rest are covered by OECD regional products (Western Balkans Competitiveness Outlook, SME Policy Index).
  • Source-type bias: IMF/WB/OECD reports are macro-structural, so defence and frontier tech are under-covered by construction — reported as gaps, not absences of fact.
  • Indirect evidence: the reports describe the candidate countries; the EU-competitiveness link is supplied by each row’s coded mechanism (inspect and contest it in evidence.csv).
  • Extraction: quotes are pypdf-extracted (occasional spacing artefacts). A few World Bank docs surfaced by country-name queries were about other countries; coding dropped those off-country passages.
