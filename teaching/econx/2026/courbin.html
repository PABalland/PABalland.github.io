What lies beneath

According to Ukrainian think tanks (cited by Reuters) and ISW frontline data, approximately 40% of Ukraine's metals and 33–50% of critical minerals are in Russian-controlled or occupied territory. (Euronews 2025 · ISW Feb 2025)
Sources: Euronews (Mar 2025) · ISW Frontline Data (Feb 2025) · Ukrainian Freedom Fund (Feb 2026) · Mind Maps analysis

Russia's geological survey plan: Ukraine's foreign intelligence service (HUR) has confirmed that Russia plans to conduct geological exploration at 70 sites in occupied territories — with priority given to lithium, graphite, manganese, molybdenum, tungsten, titanium and uranium. The goal: finance the long-term war effort using stolen Ukrainian resources.

Source: Ukrainian Foreign Intelligence Service (HUR) · Odessa Journal · CIRSD "The Mineral Wars" (Feb 2, 2026)

The Zaporizhzhia paradox: Zaporizhzhia oblast holds approximately 20% of the world's titanium reserves and one of Ukraine's two accessible lithium deposits (Kruta Balka). The region is partially occupied, and the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant — Europe's largest — has been under Russian control since March 2022. The intersection of energy, minerals, and military strategy in this single oblast is unparalleled.

Source: Euronews (Mar 2025) · NATO ENSEC COE (Jan 2026) · WEF Critical Raw Materials Report (Jul 2024)
Section 02

The deal that changed everything

On April 30, 2025, the US and Ukraine signed the Reconstruction Investment Fund agreement — the most consequential economic partnership in the conflict's history. What it actually says is far more nuanced than headlines suggest.

October 2024
Zelensky introduces minerals into his "Victory Plan"
Ukraine's president proposes a "special agreement on joint investment and use" of natural resources — uranium, titanium, lithium — with the EU and US as part of a post-war security framework. France's defence minister enters bilateral talks. (Wikipedia · Kyiv Independent)
Feb 10, 2025
Trump demands $500 billion in rare earths from Ukraine
In a public statement, Trump says the US should receive $500B worth of rare earths as compensation for military aid. Geologists later confirm Ukraine has no proven rare earth reserves. (EU Parliament · Carnegie Endowment)
Feb 28, 2025
Oval Office confrontation derails first signing
Zelensky's Washington visit ends in a televised confrontation with Trump and Vance. The minerals deal signing — scheduled for that day — is cancelled. Military aid is briefly paused. (CBS News · Newsweek)
April 30, 2025
US–Ukraine Reconstruction Investment Fund signed
Ukraine's Economy Minister Svyrydenko and US Treasury Secretary Bessent sign the deal. It lists 55 minerals. Key terms: Ukraine retains full ownership; US gets priority access to new investment projects; profits reinvested in Ukraine for first decade. No security guarantees. (CBS News · PBS · Treasury Dept)
June 2025
Russia seizes Shevchenko lithium deposit, Donetsk
The most concentrated lithium deposit in Ukraine — already part of the deal's portfolio — is captured by Russian forces during the summer offensive. The deal's premise immediately begins to erode. (NYT · West Hawaii Today)
Feb 2026
CIRSD analysis: minerals are central to Russia's war strategy
"Beneath the surface of military maneuvers and episodic peacemaking lies a deeper and often overlooked driver of the armed conflict: Ukraine's immense natural resources." Russia is occupying precisely the most mineral-rich territories — and planning 70 geological surveys. (CIRSD "The Mineral Wars," Feb 2 2026)

The strategic logic: Ukraine signed this deal not because of the economics — the actual revenue potential is modest and long-term — but because it gives the US a financial stake in Ukraine's survival. As SIPRI (May 2025) notes, the bulk of near-term value is in hydrocarbons, not rare earths. But that misses the point: the deal is a geopolitical insurance policy, not a mining contract.

Section 03

Myth vs. reality

The narrative around Ukraine's minerals has been dramatically inflated for political purposes. Here is what primary sources and peer-reviewed research actually say — and where the genuine value lies.

Claim
Ukraine holds $500 billion in rare earth elements that the US should receive as repayment for military aid. (Trump, Feb 10 2025)
Source of claim: President Trump, Feb 10 2025 · Repeated in media
Reality
Ukraine has no proven rare earth reserves. The $500B figure was, according to Carnegie Endowment, "plucked out of thin air." The global market for all key critical minerals combined — cobalt, copper, graphite, lithium, nickel, AND rare earths — is less than $500B annually.
Carnegie Endowment for International Peace (Apr 22 2025) · SIPRI Essay (May 12 2025)
Claim
Ukraine holds $14.8–$15 trillion in total mineral wealth, making it one of the richest mineral countries in the world.
Source: Ukrainian Freedom Fund · Senator Graham · Various media
Reality
These figures confuse resources (geologically present) with reserves (economically viable to extract). No modern geological survey of Ukraine's rare earth and lithium deposits has been conducted. S&P Global's mining database lists zero current projects in development for either rare earths or lithium in Ukraine. Much of what's there is in occupied territory, at depth, or in war zones.
Carnegie Endowment (Apr 2025) · SIPRI (May 2025) · bne IntelliNews (Mar 2025)
Claim
The US–Ukraine minerals deal gives Washington access to vast rare earth supplies that will reduce China dependency.
Source: Various US officials · Media coverage
Reality
Where genuine value exists is in titanium (7% of world reserves, aerospace-grade), graphite (20% of global resources — critical for EV batteries), and uranium. These are real and significant. The SIPRI analysis confirms: "Ukrainian production of graphite, manganese and titanium are and will continue to be important for mitigating the vulnerability of Western supply chains."
SIPRI (May 2025) · WEF (Jul 2024) · NATO ENSEC COE (Jan 2026)

What's genuinely strategic: Graphite (20% of global resources — EV batteries, nuclear reactors), titanium (7% of world reserves — aerospace, defence, implants), uranium (270,000 tonnes — nuclear power), and manganese (world's 2nd-largest reserves — steel, batteries). These four minerals, if Ukraine can survive the war and build processing infrastructure, represent a genuine long-run opportunity. But the timeline is decades, not years.

WEF Critical Raw Materials Report (Jul 2024) · Metal Tech News (Feb 2025) · EU Parliament EPRS (Mar 2025)

Section 05

What complexity theory explains

Standard trade economics cannot explain why having mineral deposits doesn't automatically translate into wealth, or why Russia controls the richest zones. Economic Complexity theory — from Harvard's Growth Lab — provides the framework.

ECI 2024 — Harvard Growth Lab
Countries best positioned to process Ukraine's minerals into finished products. ECI predicts who captures value in post-war reconstruction.

Harvard Growth Lab Atlas of Economic Complexity 10.0 (Sep 2024)
Ukraine ECI vs. mineral processing readiness
Ukraine's ECI (≈0.35–0.40) is modest — reflecting wartime disruption and deindustrialisation. It has ore and deposits, but limited processing capability. The complexity gap is critical: deposits only become wealth when processing capability exists.

Harvard Growth Lab (2024) · CSIS (2025) · WEF (2024). Processing readiness = composite index of smelting/refining capacity, energy infrastructure, skilled labour, ECI sub-scores.
🕸
Product Space
Deposits ≠ Capabilities
Ukraine has ore. It largely lacks the processing infrastructure to transform ore into aerospace-grade titanium sponge, battery-grade graphite, or refined lithium compounds. The Product Space (Hidalgo & Hausmann 2009) predicts this gap precisely: Ukraine's existing industrial capabilities are not proximate to high-value mineral processing in the product space.
Hidalgo & Hausmann (2009) PNAS · Harvard Growth Lab Atlas 10.0
📐
New Theory (Feb 2026)
The Joint Factor Problem
Hidalgo & Stojkoski's new mechanistic theory (arXiv 2506.18829, revised Feb 7 2026) shows ECI depends on the joint probability of holding all required production factors. Ukraine has one factor (ore) but lacks others (energy infrastructure, processing plants, skilled technicians). Post-war reconstruction must solve all simultaneously.
Hidalgo & Stojkoski arXiv 2506.18829v6 (Feb 7, 2026)
⚔️
Adaptive Resource Geopolitics
Russia's Complexity Strategy
A 2025 ScienceDirect paper introduces the concept of "adaptive resource geopolitics": Russia is occupying mineral-rich territories to sustain its influence in a post-fossil-fuel world. The overlay of territorial control onto Ukraine's resource map shows over 50% of critical minerals under Russian control — suggesting deliberate strategic resource seizure.
ScienceDirect (Mar 26, 2025) · CIRSD "The Mineral Wars" (Feb 2, 2026)
🏗️
Reconstruction Economics
The Complexity Ladder
For Ukraine to extract value from its deposits post-war, it must climb the complexity ladder: from ore extraction → processing → refined products → advanced manufacturing. Each step requires capabilities currently absent or war-damaged. The Stojkoski & Hidalgo (2025) "Optimising Economic Complexity" framework provides the mathematical roadmap for this ascent.
Stojkoski & Hidalgo arXiv 2503.04476 (Sep 2025) · Balland et al. Research Policy (2022)
🤝
Strategic Partnership Logic
Why the US Deal Makes Sense
From a complexity standpoint, the US–Ukraine fund is rational: the US provides the processing capability and capital; Ukraine provides the ore. The joint value exceeds what either can achieve alone. This is precisely the relatedness logic of Balland et al. (2022): economic complexity grows fastest when capabilities are combined across partners with complementary, not identical, productive knowledge.
Balland et al. (2022) Research Policy 51(3) · Columbia University CGEP (May 2025)
📍
Spatial Complexity
Urban Knowledge Concentration
Balland et al. (2020, Nature Human Behaviour) show complex economic activities concentrate in cities. Ukraine's mineral processing capacity was concentrated in Donetsk (now largely destroyed or occupied) and Zaporizhzhia (partially occupied). Rebuilding this must be city-specific, not just national. Dnipro and Lviv have the highest remaining industrial complexity.
Balland et al. (2020) Nature Human Behaviour 4:248–254
Section 06

What the peace deal means for minerals

With London talks confirmed for April 23 and a US proposal reportedly including recognition of Crimea's annexation, the peace process will directly determine how much of Ukraine's mineral wealth can actually be developed. Economic complexity theory makes specific predictions.

The recognition scenario: If a peace deal recognises Russian control over Donetsk, Zaporizhzhia, Luhansk, Kherson and Crimea, Russia would legally control approximately 40–50% of Ukraine's metals and critical minerals, including the Shevchenko lithium site (already seized), the Kruta Balka lithium site (Zaporizhzhia), and 20% of global titanium reserves. The US–Ukraine fund would operate on a significantly reduced resource base. (ISW · Euronews · Ukrainian think tanks via Reuters)

ISW Feb 2025 · Euronews Mar 2025 · WSJ Apr 20, 2026 (US proposal leak) · Kyiv Independent Apr 21, 2026

The complexity prediction: Even under the best-case scenario for Ukraine, extracting value from mineral deposits will take 15–25 years of deliberate industrial policy. Carnegie Endowment (Apr 2025) is blunt: "There are no current projects in any phase of development in Ukraine for either rare earths or lithium." The war has destroyed 50% of power generation capacity — mining is the most energy-intensive industrial sector. The minerals deal is about the 2040s, not the 2020s.

Carnegie Endowment (Apr 2025) · CSIS (May 2025) · bne IntelliNews (Mar 2025)

The bottom line — economic complexity perspective: Ukraine's minerals are real but their value is conditional on four things that don't currently exist simultaneously: peace, energy infrastructure, processing capability, and capital. The US–Ukraine deal is a step toward the capital condition. Economic complexity theory predicts that without the others, deposits remain inert. The war is not just destroying lives — it is destroying the industrial complexity that would make the minerals economically viable. Every day the conflict continues reduces Ukraine's post-war ECI, making recovery harder. The minerals deal creates a financial incentive for the US to help end the conflict — but that incentive must be strong enough to overcome the geopolitical obstacles on display in London on April 23.
