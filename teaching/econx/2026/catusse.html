What is the 4-Day Work Week?

The concept is simple: work the same number of hours in 4 days instead of 5, or reduce total hours worked. But implementations and results vary greatly by country, sector and approach.

⏱️

Model 1 — Compressed

Same 35–40h spread across 4 days (e.g. 4×9h). No salary cut. Common in the US and UK trials. Risk: longer days increase fatigue.

📉

Model 2 — Reduced Hours

Truly working fewer total hours (4×8h = 32h). Same salary maintained. This is the model tested in Iceland (2015-2019) and New Zealand.

🔄

Model 3 — Staggered Teams

Teams alternate days off to maintain 5-day coverage. Used in Japan (Panasonic, Hitachi). Keeps service continuity without overtime.

💼

The 100:80:100 Principle

100% pay, 80% time, 100% productivity. Proposed by the 4 Day Week Global campaign, which ran pilots in the UK, US, Ireland, Australia, and New Zealand.

Key distinction: France already has the 35-hour legal week, one of the shortest in the OECD. This makes the debate different from, say, the UK or US — French workers aren't starting from a 40h or 45h baseline. The relevant question is whether compressing those 35h into 4 days brings added value.
Historical Context

1926 — FORD, USA

The 5-Day Week is Born

Henry Ford introduces the 5-day, 40-hour week at Ford Motor Company, arguing that well-rested workers are more productive — and can shop more on weekends.

1998 — FRANCE

The Aubry Laws — 35-Hour Week

France becomes one of the first countries to legally cap the work week at 35 hours, aiming to reduce unemployment by encouraging job-sharing. Controversial from the start.

2007 — NEW ZEALAND

Perpetual Guardian Pilot

Perpetual Guardian (a trust management company) trials a 4-day week with no salary reduction. Productivity rises 20%, stress drops 7%. The concept goes global.

2015–2019 — ICELAND

The Largest Pilot in History

Iceland runs a massive public-sector trial covering ~2,500 workers (1% of the workforce). Results: same or better productivity, dramatic wellbeing improvements. 86% of Icelandic workers now work shorter or more flexible hours.

2022 — UK & IRELAND

61-Company Pilot — Landmark Results

61 British companies and their ~2,900 employees trial the 4-day week for 6 months. 92% of companies continue. Revenue stays flat or improves. Staff sick days fall 65%. Only 3 out of 61 companies revert.

2023–2024 — AUSTRALIA & EUROPE

Growing Momentum

Australia launches its own pilot (with 26 companies). Germany, Spain, and Belgium experiment at the public policy level. Belgium gives workers the legal right to compress hours into 4 days.

2024 — FRANCE

Still No National Policy

Some French companies experiment voluntarily (LDLC, Welcome to the Jungle, etc.), but there is no national pilot or legislative initiative. The debate remains theoretical in France's political sphere.

Consequences & Trade-Offs

The 4-day week is not a simple win. It triggers complex economic, social and organisational effects — some positive, some negative, and many interdependent.

✅ Potential Benefits

+Better mental health & reduced burnout. The UK pilot found stress dropped in 39% of employees. Long-term absenteeism could fall.
+Work-life balance improvements. More time for family, leisure, sport — key for parents and caregivers (often women).
+Talent attraction & retention. Companies offering 4-day weeks report fewer resignations and higher employee satisfaction scores.
+Environmental gains. Fewer commutes = lower CO₂ emissions. Some studies estimate a 20-30% reduction in commute-related carbon per worker.
+Productivity maintained or gained. Most pilots show output stays flat or rises, due to reduced meetings, better focus, and higher morale.
+Gender equality improvement. More flexible time reduces the domestic burden on women and allows more equal parenting.

⚠️ Real Risks

−Lower purchasing power if hours = wages. If the 4-day model reduces hours AND pay, workers earning minimum wage cannot afford it.
−Sector incompatibility. Healthcare, emergency services, logistics, retail, and agriculture cannot simply "compress" work — coverage gaps emerge.
−Organizational complexity. SMEs and VSEs (which represent 99% of French businesses) often lack the management capacity to reorganize schedules.
−Customer service reduction. If a company is only open Mon-Thu, clients and partners lose a day of access — competitive disadvantage in global markets.
−Inequality risk. Not all jobs are compatible: white-collar knowledge workers benefit far more than factory workers or gig workers.
−Longer days = fatigue. In compressed models (4×9h), fatigue and concentration issues can increase, offsetting wellbeing gains.
Economic Impact — Key Indicators

📊 Impact on Purchasing Power ▼
The critical distinction: Purchasing power is only threatened when hours AND salary are reduced simultaneously. In the "100:80:100" model (100% pay, 80% time), wages are fully maintained — so there is no negative income effect for workers.

However, if companies can't maintain productivity and absorb extra costs, they may freeze wages, reduce hiring, or pass costs to consumers via prices. A 2022 OFCE (French Economic Observatory) study estimated that a full 4-day week without salary reduction would cost French businesses approximately €70-90 billion/year in direct labor-equivalents — roughly 3% of GDP — without accounting for productivity gains offsetting this.

Minimum-wage workers are especially vulnerable: if the model reduces total hours (e.g. from 35h to 32h), even with proportional pay, a worker at SMIC (€1,767/month in 2024) would lose about €160/month — a meaningful reduction for low-income households.
🏭 Organisational Challenges for French SMEs ▼
France's economy is dominated by TPEs and PMEs (small and micro enterprises). Unlike large corporations such as Microsoft or Unilever that ran 4-day pilots, most French SMEs:

• Operate in sectors requiring 5-day physical presence (retail, crafts, construction)
• Cannot afford HR consultants to redesign work flows
• Have thin profit margins — any productivity dip is existential
• Often rely on one or two key employees whose absences cannot be covered

The "pilot bias" problem: All successful 4-day week pilots self-selected companies that were already digitally mature, had progressive management, and operated in knowledge-intensive sectors. These are not representative of the French business landscape.
⚕️ Impact on Public Services & Healthcare ▼
France's public sector — which employs approximately 5.7 million people (20% of the workforce) — faces specific challenges:

Hospitals: Already understaffed, French hospitals cannot reduce working hours without either recruiting more staff (costly) or creating coverage gaps. The 35h reform of 1998 already created significant tensions in healthcare.

Education: Teacher schedules are tied to school calendars and pupil-hours. Reducing teacher working days impacts student learning time unless compensated by pedagogical restructuring.

Transport & logistics: SNCF, RATP, La Poste — essential services that run on tight 5/6-day schedules. Any shift to 4 days requires significant investment in additional staff.
🌱 Environmental Economic Benefits ▼
Often overlooked in purely economic analyses, the environmental gains have real economic value:

• Carbon reduction: A UK study (Autonomy, 2023) estimated a 4-day week could cut UK carbon emissions by up to 127 million tonnes/year — comparable to taking 27 million cars off the road. This has economic value in the context of EU carbon targets and avoided climate-damage costs.

• Reduced office costs: Companies save on energy (heating, lighting, servers) on the 5th day. Microsoft Japan reported a 23% reduction in electricity costs during its trial.

• Reduced commuting infrastructure pressure: Fewer peak-hour commutes reduce wear on transport infrastructure and reduce urban congestion costs.

🇫🇷 France — Why So Resistant?

France already has one of the shortest legal work weeks in the OECD. Yet it remains one of the most resistant to further reduction. Several structural, cultural, and political factors explain this paradox.

The French paradox: France legally works 35 hours/week — yet French workers log 1,511 hours/year on average (OECD 2022), and the country ranks low on work-life balance satisfaction. The 35h law hasn't fully delivered its promises — partly because of widespread "overtime exemptions."

Why France Doesn't Move Forward

🏛️ Political Deadlock & Social Conflict Legacy ▼
The 35h week was introduced under Lionel Jospin's socialist government — and has been a political football ever since. Right-wing governments (Sarkozy, then Macron) have sought to water it down or add exemptions, not extend it. This makes any new work-time reduction politically charged: the right sees it as anti-competitive, and even some unions fear it could become a salary-reduction tool if implemented badly.

Furthermore, France's tradition of adversarial labour relations — unlike the more consensus-based models in Scandinavia — makes large-scale experiments harder to negotiate between unions and employers.
💰 The Competitiveness Argument ▼
France competes with European neighbors who work more hours. Germany averages ~1,340h/year; France ~1,511h. But German workers are often more productive per hour — suggesting the issue isn't total hours, but efficiency, investment, and industrial organisation.

The fear: if French companies reduce hours without a matching leap in productivity, unit labor costs rise → competitiveness falls → exports suffer → jobs move abroad. This concern is especially acute in manufacturing, agri-food, and construction sectors that compete globally on price.
🤝 The Union Divide ▼
French unions are divided on the 4-day week:

• CFDT (reformist): Open to negotiated models, case-by-case, if salary is protected. Favors sectoral agreements.
• CGT (radical left): Supports work-time reduction in principle, but wary of employer opportunism — fears companies will use "4 days" as cover to cut jobs or intensify work.
• FO, CFE-CGC: Split opinions. Some support compressed models for white-collar workers; others prioritize salary.

Without union consensus, a national reform is extremely difficult in France's highly unionized public sector and heavily regulated private sector.
🏥 The 35h Trauma in Healthcare ▼
The 1998-2002 implementation of the 35h week in hospitals created lasting tensions in French healthcare. Hospitals had to restructure shifts, often without enough extra staff being hired. The result: organisational complexity increased, staff morale fell in some sectors, and waiting lists grew.

This "trauma" makes French hospital administrators and doctors extremely resistant to any further reduction. It's often cited as proof that blanket work-time reduction laws fail in complex operational environments — even if they work in offices.

💡 What Could Be Done Instead?

If a blanket 4-day week is impractical in France right now, there are credible intermediate and alternative policies — each with distinct economic and social logics.

🧪

National Pilot Program

Like Germany's 2024 pilot: fund 50-100 volunteer companies across sectors for 6 months. Collect real French data. Avoid ideological debate by letting evidence lead. Cost: ~€20-30M — a fraction of other labour policy budgets.

📋

Sectoral Agreements (Branch-Level)

Rather than a national law, allow sectors to negotiate 4-day week arrangements via collective bargaining. The Belgian model gives workers a right to compress hours — without imposing it on all employers simultaneously.

🏢

Right to Request (Like UK)

Give all French employees a legal right to request flexible or compressed working arrangements. Employers can refuse with justification. Low regulatory burden; preserves company flexibility while expanding worker rights.

📈

Productivity-Linked Reduction

Tie any work-time reduction to verified productivity gains. If a company demonstrates it can maintain output with 32h, it can shift its entire team. Incentivizes management innovation rather than imposing blanket rules.

🎓

Training Day Model

Use a freed "5th day" for mandatory upskilling and retraining — critical given AI-driven automation. Employees gain skills; companies gain adaptability; government advances its "France 2030" competitiveness goals.

⚖️

Enforce Existing Law Better

Before reducing hours, France could enforce its 35h laws more rigorously. The "forfait jours" loophole means millions of salaried managers work 45-55h with no overtime. Closing this gap first is lower-hanging fruit.

Key insight: The countries that succeeded didn't impose a 4-day week — they enabled it through legal frameworks, funding for pilots, and sectoral flexibility. France's top-down legislative culture is ill-suited to the bottom-up experimentation that made Iceland, New Zealand, and the UK successful.

🔗 Everything is Connected

The 4-day work week debate doesn't exist in isolation. It connects to France's retirement age crisis, gender equality, automation, urban planning, and demographic challenges — a complex system where every lever affects others.

The Retirement Link — France's Blind Spot

The paradox: French people fiercely reject raising the retirement age (massive protests in 2023 against Macron's reform moving it from 62 to 64) — yet simultaneously resist reducing working hours. The underlying tension: France needs workers to work more total hours over their lifetime to fund its pension system, while also wanting better work-life balance. These goals are arithmetically in conflict.
🔴 Retirement Age × Work Week: The Impossible Triangle ▼
France's pay-as-you-go pension system (retraite par répartition) requires a sufficient ratio of active workers to retirees. As the population ages:

• In 2000: ~4 workers per retiree → comfortable funding
• In 2023: ~1.7 workers per retiree → structural deficit
• Projected 2040: ~1.4 workers per retiree without reform

The triangle: France cannot simultaneously (a) keep the retirement age at 62, (b) reduce weekly working hours, AND (c) maintain current pension levels — without either raising taxes or increasing debt. Something must give. Countries like New Zealand that successfully implement 4-day weeks tend to have different pension structures (more funded, less pay-as-you-go) or higher immigration rates that maintain the worker ratio.

In other countries: New Zealand has a universal pension (NZ Super) funded via general taxation, not contribution records — making it less sensitive to hours worked. This structural difference is rarely mentioned in French debates about copying the NZ model.
🤖 Automation: The Hidden Enabler (or Threat) ▼
AI and automation are changing the productivity equation. If machines increasingly handle routine tasks, human workers can produce more output in fewer hours — making the 4-day week economically viable.

The optimistic view: Rising automation justifies work-time reduction. In a world where a factory robot does what 3 workers did, society can afford for those workers to work 4 days and share the gains in leisure time rather than unemployment.

The pessimistic view: Automation disproportionately eliminates lower-skill jobs. A 4-day week benefits knowledge workers who can leverage AI tools — but leaves manual workers, delivery drivers, and retail staff facing precarity, not leisure. Without redistribution mechanisms, automation + 4-day week = deeper inequality.

France's "France 2030" industrial strategy invests heavily in automation but has no corresponding work-time policy — a significant gap.
♀ Gender Equality: The Underrated Connection ▼
In France, women perform on average 3h24 more unpaid domestic/care work per day than men (INSEE, 2023). A 4-day work week — especially with a fixed day off — could be transformative:

• More time for both parents → more equal distribution of domestic work
• Reduced reliance on costly childcare for one extra day
• Women returning to work more easily post-childbirth if schedules are flexible

The Iceland pilot specifically documented improvements in gender equality as a key outcome. Iceland already ranks #1 globally on the Global Gender Gap Index — and credits flexible work policies as a contributing factor.

France ranks 15th on the same index (2023). Better work scheduling could accelerate progress, but only if men also take the free day for domestic responsibilities — which requires cultural as well as structural change.

France's unique combination of a contested 64-year retirement age, a stressed pay-as-you-go pension, adversarial social relations, and an already-short 35h week makes it one of the most structurally difficult environments to add a further work-time reduction — regardless of how desirable it might be socially.
