The core problem

Medicine was built for the "standard" male body

For most of modern medical history, the 70kg white male was used as the default model for clinical research, drug development, and diagnostic criteria. This wasn't a conspiracy — it was path dependency. Early choices about who to study shaped what tools got built, which shaped what researchers knew how to do, which shaped the next round of funding. Women's bodies were systematically excluded, and the knowledge gap compounded quietly for decades.

Why it matters now

The gap has consequences that compound over time

When women's health is underfunded, it doesn't just affect medicine — it shapes careers, families, economic productivity, and quality of life for billions. Conditions like endometriosis, PCOS, and autoimmune diseases (which disproportionately affect women) remain poorly understood decades after discovery.

The thalidomide shadow

One tragedy shaped 40 years of exclusion

Thalidomide was a sedative widely used across Europe and Canada in the late 1950s and early 1960s. Thousands of women who took it while pregnant gave birth to babies with severe limb deformities. Although it was never approved in the United States, the tragedy sent shockwaves through the medical research establishment.

The response was the sweeping 1977 FDA policy excluding women of childbearing potential from Phase I and early Phase II drug trials — a definition so broad it included women using contraception, single women, and women whose husbands had been vasectomized. Well-intentioned but blunt, this response created a scientific blind spot that would take nearly two decades to begin to correct.

The "Bikini medicine" critique

Research reduced women to reproductive anatomy

The term "bikini medicine" was coined in the early 1980s by American cardiologist Dr. Nanette Wenger to describe how women's health research was largely limited to the areas covered by a bikini — breasts and reproductive organs. When women were finally studied, research overwhelmingly focused on pregnancy, fertility, and gynecological conditions. Heart disease, stroke risk, chronic pain, and mental health in women remained deeply understudied despite being leading causes of mortality and disability.

Root causes of the gap

The underinvestment is systemic — not accidental.

Structural

Who funds and who decides

Medical research funding decisions are made predominantly by men. While women represented 40.1% of NIH study section reviewers between 2019 and 2021, their representation among chairpersons did not improve over the same period — meaning women remain underrepresented at the level where funding priorities are most directly shaped. (PubMed, 2025) Funding bodies, pharmaceutical boards, and academic tenure committees all skew heavily male — leading to unconscious prioritization of male-pattern disease presentations. The knowledge that gets produced reflects the people producing it.

Scientific

A knowledge base built on the wrong foundation

Decades of male-default research created a dense, self-reinforcing cluster of tools, biomarkers, dosage protocols, and diagnostic criteria — all calibrated to male physiology. Women's health, by contrast, sits in a sparse corner of the medical knowledge space, surrounded by few related capabilities to build on. The infrastructure simply was never laid.

Economic

Thin ecosystems look like bad investments

When the surrounding research ecosystem is thin — few specialists trained, few tools available, little prior data — investment looks risky regardless of the underlying need. A McKinsey analysis found that 11 startups addressing erectile dysfunction secured $1.24 billion in 2019–23, while 8 startups addressing endometriosis received just $44 million — funding for erectile dysfunction was six times greater. However, the tide may be turning: women's health newcomers received $2.2 billion in funding over the same period, with 60% of top deals exclusively addressing women's health.

Pain is dismissed differently in women +

A 2024 PNAS study conducted across US and Israeli emergency departments found robust evidence that female patients are consistently less likely to be prescribed pain relief than male patients reporting the same level of pain. Women are more likely to be prescribed sedatives rather than analgesics — a disparity rooted in centuries of medical literature portraying women as more emotionally reactive and less reliable reporters of their own symptoms. The consequences are twofold: women receive worse care in the short term, and in the long term, conditions that disproportionately cause pain in women attract less research funding — because if pain is seen as psychosomatic, it won't be taken seriously as a scientific problem worth solving.

"Hysteria" and the long shadow of dismissal +

The word "hysteria" derives from the Greek word for womb, and its history is more complex than it might seem. As historical analysis shows, ancient Greek physicians actually viewed hysteria-related symptoms as real and organic — not imaginary. Over centuries, however, the concept evolved into a catch-all diagnosis applied almost exclusively to women, used to explain a wide range of unexplained symptoms as psychological or exaggerated. By the 19th and 20th centuries, "hysteria" had become a tool of dismissal. Its legacy persists today: a 2002 qualitative study found that women with chronic fatigue syndrome and fibromyalgia experienced significant stigmatization, with their symptoms being psychologized and their veracity questioned — primarily before receiving a diagnosis.

The "too complicated" argument +

Hormonal fluctuations across the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause make women "harder" to study in controlled trials. Rather than developing better methodology, the field chose exclusion. This is scientifically unjustifiable — and actually means we know far less about how drugs interact with female physiology at different life stages.

Reproductive framing narrows the scope +

When women's health is funded, it's overwhelmingly focused on reproductive and maternal health. While these are critical, they leave enormous gaps: cardiovascular disease (the #1 killer of women), mental health, musculoskeletal conditions, and sex-specific differences in cancer progression all remain understudied in female populations.

What the gap costs us

Underinvestment has real, measurable consequences for health, economies, and lives.

Health outcomes

Women are diagnosed later, treated less effectively

Because heart attack symptoms in women differ from the "classic" male presentation, women have a 50% higher chance of receiving an initial wrong diagnosis following a heart attack — which can have potentially devastating consequences for their survival. Women with conditions like lupus or endometriosis wait years — sometimes decades — for a correct diagnosis.

Drug safety

Drugs approved for men, prescribed to everyone

Women metabolize drugs differently — lower kidney filtration rates, different body fat distribution, hormonal variations. When trials don't include women, dosage guidelines become male-optimized by default. A 2001 US Government Accountability Office report found that 8 out of 10 prescription drugs withdrawn from the US market between 1997 and 2000 posed greater health risks for women than for men.

Economic & social cost

The trillion-dollar blind spot

According to the McKinsey Global Institute, a woman will spend an average of nine years in poor health — affecting her ability to be present and productive at home, in the workforce, and in the community, and reducing her earning potential. Addressing this gap could add at least $1 trillion annually to the global economy by 2040. The cost of inaction is staggering:

The path forward

Change is possible — and already happening. Here's what it looks like.

Policy

Mandate, don't suggest

The NIH's 2016 policy requiring sex as a biological variable in preclinical research was a step — but enforcement remains weak. Strong mandatory inclusion policies with real consequences for non-compliance are the most proven lever for systemic change. Without mandates, the research ecosystem continues to reproduce what it already knows how to do.

Research

Build the adjacent capabilities first

You can't close a knowledge gap by injecting money alone — you need to grow the surrounding ecosystem. That means training specialists, developing sex-specific biomarkers, building dedicated trial infrastructure, and creating research clusters where women's health sits close enough to existing strengths to attract the next generation of scientists.

Investment

The market is finally paying attention

According to a McKinsey Global Institute analysis, women's health newcomers received $2.2 billion in funding over 2019–2023, with 60% of top deals exclusively addressing women's health — specifically endometriosis, fertility, and maternal and neonatal health. Startups tackling these conditions are attracting serious capital for the first time. Once an ecosystem reaches critical mass, investment follows.

Education & Prevention — McKinsey Global Institute

Awareness is medicine too

Around the world, women experiencing conditions such as painful periods, endometriosis, PCOS, or uterine fibroids may have limited awareness of what is normal and when to seek medical advice. Better health education can improve outcomes, reduce symptom severity, and prevent future complications.

Prevention matters too. The HPV vaccine is proven to reduce the incidence of cervical cancer by nearly 90% — yet less than 25% of low-income countries and less than 30% of lower-middle-income countries have introduced it, compared with 85% of high-income countries. Only 36% of women worldwide have ever been screened for cervical cancer — 84% in high-income countries, but less than 20% in lower-income ones.

The awareness gap extends to physicians too: many doctors remain unaware of how diseases manifest differently in women, leaving them unable to provide adequate care to a large share of their patients.

Accessibility & Affordability — McKinsey Global Institute

Women pay more for less care

Healthcare spending and insurance premiums have historically been higher for women. In Switzerland, women pay more than 12% extra for supplementary hospital insurance on average — with a 31-year-old woman paying 37% more than a man of the same age. Indian private insurers similarly employ gender-based premiums. In the US, women have an average of $135 more out-of-pocket expenses per year than men, of which $55 goes to higher copay rates for conditions predominantly affecting women.

Affordability extends beyond healthcare services. Around 500 million people worldwide lack access to menstrual products and hygiene facilities. In Bangladesh, a study by the HERproject found that 73% of women employed by a textile factory missed work for an average of six days a month due to menstrual health issues — absenteeism that dropped to just 3% when menstrual pads and health information were provided.

What you can do

As a patient

Track your symptoms and cycles in detail. Bring data to appointments. Ask for sex-specific research on any medication prescribed. Seek second opinions if dismissed. Organizations like The Endometriosis Foundation, PCOS Challenge, and The Menopause Society offer research-backed resources.

As a citizen

Support candidates and policies that fund women's health research. Advocate for your workplace to cover comprehensive women's health benefits. Amplify researchers doing this work. The conversation is shifting — public pressure has historically been the most effective driver of funding reform in healthcare.
